Why Use Both Vendi and Persistent Homology?

When evaluating the diversity and structure of high-dimensional data—especially medical image embeddings— it's easy to default to simple metrics like distance or variance. But those often miss the deeper story. By combining Vendi entropy and Persistent Homology, we get a more complete picture: one metric tells us how spread out the data is, the other reveals how it's shaped.

✅ Why This Combination Works

  • Two Lenses, One Dataset: Vendi reveals statistical entropy; PH uncovers loops, clusters, and shape complexity.
  • Unsupervised and Interpretable: Neither metric requires labels or downstream tasks—they describe the data directly.
  • Spotting Subtle Structure: Datasets that look similar by entropy may diverge in topology, flagging unseen variation.
  • Educational Clarity: A compelling way to teach that not all “diverse” datasets are structurally rich—and vice versa.

⚠️ What to Keep in Mind

  • Scale Sensitivity: Persistent Homology can be sensitive to density and scale without preprocessing or normalization.
  • Computational Cost: PH computations scale poorly with dimensionality and size—though approximate methods help.
  • Interpretability Gap: While Vendi gives a scalar score, PH produces diagrams that require expertise to interpret.
  • Not Always Aligned: Sometimes the metrics disagree—this is a feature, not a bug. It shows multiple layers of structure.

In sum, combining Vendi and Persistent Homology gives us a dual view: one statistical, one structural. Together, they make data diversity more than just a number—they make it something we can see and explore.
